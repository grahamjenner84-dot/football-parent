## Why Categories Exist

Under the Elite Player Performance Plan (EPPP), every professional academy is independently audited and awarded a category from 1 to 4. The academy category reflects the club's coaching provision, staffing, facilities, education and player support rather than the ability of the children who play there. The audit covers:

- Quality and availability of training facilities
- Number of qualified coaches and support staff
- Coaching hours delivered per week at each phase 
- Player welfare, safeguarding and education provision
- Medical and sports science support

The amount of contact time also changes as players move through the Foundation Phase, Youth Development Phase and Professional Development Phase. Our guide to [What Is EPPP?](/academy-pathway/what-is-eppp) explains how these stages fit together.

## Category 1

The highest level, held by Premier League clubs and some well-resourced Championship clubs. Category 1 academies must deliver the most contact hours, employ full-time coaching staff across all phases, provide high-quality training facilities, and run formal education support alongside football.

**What this means for families:** The commitment is significant - multiple sessions per week, weekend matches, and travel are standard. 

Catchment and recruitment rules vary by category and matter practically for families in London and other areas with multiple clubs nearby. Category 1 academies can recruit players nationally from Under-12 upwards - they are not restricted to a local catchment at that age. 

Categories 2, 3 and 4 work differently: Categories 2 and 3 can only register players who live within a 90-minute travel radius of the training ground (Under-12 to Under-16), and within 60 minutes for Under-9 to Under-11. 

Category 4 is exempt from these restrictions entirely, since it primarily operates at Under-17 and above, where the travel rules don't apply in the same way. This means that if your child is Under-11, a Category 2 or 3 club more than 60 minutes away cannot approach them, even if they wanted to - while a Category 1 club further away could. 

Although academy coaching itself is free regardless of category, the overall commitment can still vary significantly depending on training frequency, travel and match schedules. Before accepting an offer, it's worth understanding [how much academy football costs](/academy-pathway/how-much-does-academy-football-cost) in practice.

Category 1 is often described as the pinnacle of English youth development. But a large, competitive squad can also mean less individual attention and less game time for players who are not among the clear top performers in the group.

## Category 2

Typically Championship or upper-League One clubs. Standards remain high - staffing, facilities and hours are all substantial - but slightly less intensive than Category 1.

**What this means for families:** A significant commitment, but often with slightly less travel pressure and, depending on squad size, more individual attention for developing players. For many players, a Category 2 environment is an excellent fit - a professional setting without the sheer competitive volume of the largest Category 1 programmes.

## Category 3

Usually found at lower Championship and League One clubs. Training hours are lower, facilities more modest, staffing leaner.

**What this means for families:** Lower travel demands and training frequency. Reduced squad competition often means more playing time and more individual focus from coaches.

Category 3 academies are sometimes dismissed unfairly. Many very good coaches work at this level, and players who progress through a Category 3 programme can receive substantial individual development attention. The pathway is less resource-heavy, but it suits some players well.

## Category 4

The minimum EPPP standard, and structurally different from the categories above it. Where Categories 1–3 run full academy pathways from Under-9 upward, Category 4 operates primarily as a late-development model focused on the Professional Development Phase (Under-17 to Under-21). It is not designed to develop young children from foundation age. 

Parents of players in their early teens or below should be aware of this distinction before assuming Category 4 is simply a lower-resource version of the full academy system, it serves a different purpose entirely.

In practice, Category 4 clubs use the programme to develop older players who may have been released elsewhere, picked up from semi-professional football, or identified as late developers. Hours are at their lowest within the EPPP framework, staffing is lean, and facilities are more basic. Some Category 4 clubs run development centres alongside their formal academy as a way of widening their scouting pool without the overhead of a larger registered programme.

It's also worth noting that Category 4 academies are exempt from the EPPP catchment distance restrictions that apply to Categories 2 and 3. Since Category 4 primarily works with players aged 17 and above, the travel radius rules designed to protect younger children don't apply in the same way.

**What this means for families:** If your child is under 16, a Category 4 academy is unlikely to be actively recruiting them into a registered programme. If your child is 17 or older and has been released from another academy, Category 4 can be a genuine and legitimate route back into the professional development pathway. 

Category 4 is still a properly governed, EPPP-registered programme, it is not the same as a development centre. Our guide to [development centres vs academies](/academy-pathway/development-centres-vs-academies) explains this distinction in detail if you're unsure where a programme your child has been invited to actually sits.

## What Category Actually Means for Your Child's Development

> **Football Parent note:** The category number is often the first thing parents focus on, and it matters least at younger ages. A child receiving excellent individual coaching at a Category 3 club - with regular game time and a good relationship with their coach - may develop faster than one sitting in a large Category 1 squad. Ask clubs about squad sizes, player-to-coach ratios, and how they communicate with parents. These questions tell you more than the category number alone.

What matters more than category, particularly at Foundation and early Youth Development phase:

- The quality and consistency of the coaches working with your child
- Training and match volume for their specific age group
- How the club communicates with players and parents
- The environment and culture around the programme

Receiving an academy offer doesn't always mean your child has to stop playing grassroots football immediately. Before making any decisions, it's worth understanding [whether academy players can play grassroots football](/academy-pathway/can-academy-players-play-grassroots-football).

## Common Misconceptions

**"My child must aim for a Category 1 academy."**
Not necessarily. Individual fit, travel demands and squad competition all matter. Some players develop better in less intensive environments, particularly at younger ages.

**"Category 4 isn't a real academy."**
It is a formally EPPP-registered academy with proper governance. It is not equivalent to a development centre or commercial coaching programme. 

**"The category tells you the quality."**
Not entirely. Two Category 2 academies can have significantly different coaching cultures and player experiences. The category gives you a structural framework - it doesn't tell you everything about what it's like to be there.

## FAQ: Academy Categories

### What is a Category 1 academy?

A Category 1 academy is the highest academy level under the Elite Player Performance Plan (EPPP). These clubs meet the highest standards for coaching, facilities, education, and player support. However, a Category 1 academy is not automatically the best fit for every player.

### Can a club's category change?

Yes. Clubs are audited periodically and category status can go up or down. Don't rely on historical information - verify current status with the club or through official league sources.

### Does a higher category mean a better chance of going professional?

No. The [Premier League's own Parent Hub](https://www.premierleague.com/en/footballandcommunity/youth-development/parent-hub/the-academy-experience) publishes graduation rates by category: roughly 11% of Category 1 academy players go on to make 20 or more league appearances as professionals, compared to 6% at Category 2 and 3% at Category 3. 

These figures make two things clear: the majority of players at every category level, including Category 1, do not reach professional football; and the gap between categories is smaller than many parents assume. Category does not reliably predict individual professional outcomes. Our guide to [understanding academy release](/academy-pathway/understanding-academy-release) covers what happens and how families can best prepare.

### Is a Category 1 academy always the best option?

Not necessarily. Category 1 academies generally offer the highest levels of coaching provision, facilities and support, but they also tend to have larger squads, greater competition and higher travel demands. For many young players, a Category 2 or Category 3 academy may provide more regular playing opportunities and an environment better suited to their development. You'll know your child and whether they are ready for what can be a tough, competitive environment.

### Can Category 3 academies produce Premier League players?

Yes. Category 3 academies can and do produce professional and Premier League players. While they typically have fewer resources than Category 1 clubs, player development depends on much more than academy category alone.
