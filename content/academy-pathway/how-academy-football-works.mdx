## Quick Overview

A football academy is the youth development system run by professional football clubs to identify, coach and develop talented young players. In England, football academies operate under the Elite Player Performance Plan (EPPP), which sets standards for coaching, training, player welfare and education. This guide explains how football academy works, what families can expect and how children progress through the academy pathway.

Many parents assume academy football follows a straight pathway from Under-9 to a professional contract. Many professional clubs also run **pre-academy football** for children below academy registration age. Although these programmes can introduce players to a club's coaching environment, they are not the same as being signed by an academy.  In reality, players join and leave academies at different ages, recruitment continues throughout the teenage years, and most academy journeys look very different from what families expect when they first enter the system. In reality, clubs continue recruiting throughout childhood and the teenage years. ## What Is A Football Academy?

A football academy is the youth development programme operated by a professional football club to develop young players from childhood through to potential professional football.

Most football academies in England operate within the Elite Player Performance Plan (EPPP), which sets the rules for coaching, education, welfare and player development.

Although every club runs its academy slightly differently, they all follow the same broad pathway from Foundation Phase through to the Professional Development Phase.

## What is EPPP?

The EPPP is a regulatory framework designed to standardise youth development across English professional football. It was introduced in 2012 and governs:

- **Academy registration and categorisation** (Category 1-4, based on resources and standards)
- **Training hours** at each age group
- **Catchment areas** (geographical restrictions on where clubs can recruit from)
- **Player welfare and education** requirements
- **Coaching standards** and staff-to-player ratios

The key point: EPPP creates a structured, regulated pathway. All EPPP academies operate under the same overarching governance framework, although requirements and permitted activity vary significantly between Categories 1-4. For a deeper explanation of the rules and standards behind the system, see our full guide to [what the EPPP is and how it works](/academy-pathway/what-is-eppp).

## Foundation Phase: Under-9 to Under-11

Foundation Phase is the entry point into formal academy football. Formal academy registration begins at Under-9, but many clubs identify talented children earlier through pre-academy programmes and player development centres. These programmes give children an opportunity to develop before academy registration becomes possible, although they do not guarantee an academy place. If you're unsure how these programmes fit into the wider pathway, see [UK Football Development Centres Explained](/academy-pathway/uk-football-development-centres-explained).

One question parents often ask at this stage is whether joining an academy means leaving their grassroots team immediately. The answer depends on the player's age and the club involved. Read our guide to [whether academy players can play grassroots football](/academy-pathway/can-academy-players-play-grassroots-football) for a full explanation.

**What it looks like:**

- **Training frequency**: Varies by academy category. Some academies may offer 1-2 formal academy sessions per week, while higher-category academies usually have greater minimum contact time.
- **Recruitment**: Largely local, with EPPP restrictions limiting how far clubs can recruit from at younger ages
- **Focus**: Technical foundation, decision-making basics, enjoyment and retention
- **Matches**: Competitive but development-focused; not all-consuming

**What parents need to know:**

Being recruited at Foundation Phase usually means academy staff believe your child has long-term development potential rather than simply being one of the strongest players in local grassroots football today. It is not definitive or determinative of anything. Many players join later and catch up quickly. This is a formative, developmental stage, not a make-or-break assessment.

Children born earlier in the school year are statistically over-represented in academy football. Our guide to [Relative Age Effect In Football](/football-development/relative-age-effect-football) explains why this happens and why late developers should not assume they have missed their opportunity.

## Youth Development Phase: Under-12 to Under-16

The Youth Development Phase runs from U12 to U16 and is where competitive academy football becomes more demanding. Recruitment remains active throughout this phase, and many players enter academy football after being identified in grassroots football.

Many academies also use bio-banding at various points during this phase. Bio-banding groups players by biological maturity rather than chronological age, helping coaches assess late developers more fairly and reducing some of the advantages enjoyed by early-maturing players.

**What it looks like:**

- **Training frequency**: 2-4 academy sessions per week depending on category
- **Recruitment**: Active - many players join at U12 or U13 after being scouted from grassroots football. Our guide to [How Football Scouts Identify Players](/academy-trials/how-football-scouts-identify-players) explains what scouts typically look for at these ages.
- **Competition**: Higher level; regional or national tournaments are common
- **Focus**: Tactical understanding, position specialisation, competitive resilience
- **Selection**: U15 and U16 are major decision points, with scholarship decisions usually discussed towards the end of this phase

**What parents need to know:**

This is the phase where the gap between academy and grassroots becomes more noticeable. Talented grassroots players who have not been scouted earlier often enter the system here. Not being recruited at Foundation Phase does not close doors at Youth Development - in fact, recruitment is very active at this age.

Many players who have spent years in academy football are not retained at this stage. If your child is approaching this age group, our guide to [Understanding Academy Release](/academy-pathway/understanding-academy-release) explains what happens next and what support clubs are expected to provide.

## Professional Development Phase: Under-17 to Under-23

The Professional Development Phase (PDP) is the final academy stage before senior football. Players usually enter this phase after signing a scholarship agreement at age 16.

This is where academy football increasingly resembles a professional environment. Players train full-time or near full-time while combining football with education, apprenticeships or further qualifications.

**What it looks like:**

- **Status**: Scholarship agreement from age 16, with professional contracts available from age 17
- **Training**: Full-time or near full-time programmes
- **Competition**: U18, U21 and Premier League 2 competitions, reserve football and loan opportunities
- **Age range**: U17-U23
- **Pathways**: Professional contracts, non-league football, university football, scholarships and overseas opportunities

**What parents need to know:**

While academy football often feels highly selective, players continue to move into and out of professional pathways throughout this age range. Some players earn professional contracts, while others build successful careers through university football, semi-professional football or later recruitment opportunities. ## Academy Categories Explained

All EPPP academies are categorised 1-4 based on:

- Investment and resources
- Coaching staff quality and quantity
- Training hours and facilities
- Education and welfare provision
- Historical development outcomes

**Category 1**: Highest level of academy provision, with the greatest staffing, facilities, contact time and support services.

**Category 2**: Significant academy provision with extensive coaching, education and welfare support.

**Category 3**: Smaller academy operation with reduced contact hours and resources compared to Categories 1 and 2.

**Category 4**: Operate only in the Professional Development Phase and provide a pathway for players aged 17 and above.

Academy category is determined through the EPPP audit process and licensing requirements rather than a club's league position. While many Premier League clubs operate Category 1 academies, academy category and league status are not the same thing.

Academy category influences coaching contact time, staffing levels, facilities and support services, but it does not determine an individual player's long-term progression. For a full breakdown of what each category means in practice, see [Academy Categories Explained](/academy-pathway/academy-categories-explained).

## What Parents Should Expect From Academy Football

The academy structure is only part of the story. For many families, the biggest adjustment is the time commitment. Parents also need to consider the wider commitment beyond training sessions. Although academy coaching is usually provided free of charge, travel, equipment, tournaments and time away from work can make academy football more expensive than many families expect. Our guide to [how much academy football costs](/academy-pathway/how-much-does-academy-football-cost) explains the typical costs parents should budget for.

Depending on the club and age group, academy football can involve multiple midweek training sessions, long journeys after school, weekend fixtures across the country and regular player review meetings.

Many parents are surprised by how much organisation is required. Schoolwork, family commitments, siblings' activities and travel all need to fit around football.

Parents are also often surprised that academy pathways are rarely linear. Players are recruited at different ages, released at different stages and sometimes re-enter the system later. Being signed at U9 does not guarantee a scholarship, and not being signed at U9 does not prevent future recruitment.

Understanding the pathway is important, but understanding the realities of academy life can be just as valuable when deciding whether it is the right environment for your child.

## Academy Reviews And Player Assessments

One of the biggest surprises for many parents is how much academy football revolves around ongoing assessment rather than match-day performance alone.

Most academies hold regular player review meetings involving coaches, players and parents. The frequency varies between clubs and age groups, but reviews are typically built into the academy calendar throughout the season.

While every academy has its own process, reviews often look at several areas of development:

- Technical ability
- Tactical understanding
- Physical development
- Psychological characteristics
- Learning attitude and coachability
- School and educational progress
- Attendance, commitment and professionalism

Parents are sometimes surprised to learn that coaches are rarely judging players solely on goals scored, assists, or whether they were the best player in the last match.

At younger ages, coaches are often more interested in a player's ability to learn, adapt, solve problems and respond to coaching. A technically gifted player who struggles to take on feedback may be viewed differently from a player who improves consistently throughout the season.

Physical development can also influence assessments. Players who mature later may not dominate games at Under-12 or Under-13 level but can still be viewed as strong long-term prospects. This is one reason some academies use bio-banding and why understanding the [Relative Age Effect In Football](/football-development/relative-age-effect-football) can be helpful for parents.

### What Parents Should Ask During Reviews

Review meetings are an opportunity to gather useful information rather than simply seek reassurance.

Useful questions include:

- What are my child's biggest strengths right now?
- What are the main development priorities?
- What should they focus on between now and the next review?
- Are there any concerns around technical, tactical, physical or psychological development?
- How does the club support players who develop later physically?
- What would successful progress look like over the next six to twelve months?

Many experienced academy parents find that the most useful reviews are not the ones filled with praise. The best reviews provide clear, actionable feedback that helps a player improve.

### Understanding Release And Retention Decisions

Academy football involves continuous assessment throughout the pathway. Retention and release decisions can happen at multiple age groups rather than only at scholarship age.

For that reason, many coaches encourage families to focus on development rather than simply staying in the academy system for as long as possible.

Being retained does not guarantee future progression. Equally, being released does not close the door on future opportunities. Players regularly move between academy football, grassroots football, development centres and other performance pathways throughout their teenage years.

If your child does experience release, our guide to [Understanding Academy Release](/academy-pathway/understanding-academy-release) explains what support clubs should provide and what options remain available afterwards.

## FAQ: How Academy Football Works

**Q: What is a football academy?**

A: A football academy is the youth development system operated by a professional football club. Children receive structured coaching, regular games and ongoing development within the EPPP framework, with the aim of helping talented players reach the highest level possible.

**Q: Can my child be in an academy and grassroots football at the same time?**

A: It depends on the academy's category, the player's age group and the academy's registration rules. During Foundation Phase, many academy players continue with grassroots football. As players move into older age groups, restrictions usually become tighter and parents should confirm the exact position with the academy before committing. Our guide to [leaving grassroots football for an academy](/parent-guides/leave-grassroots-football-for-an-academy) explains the questions to ask before making that decision.

**Q: What happens if my child is struggling in the academy?**

A: Start by asking the coach for clear feedback on what your child is doing well, what needs work and whether the issue is technical, physical, tactical, confidence-related or linked to maturity. Some children need time to adapt; others benefit from stepping back to grassroots and returning later. It is common for players to move between academy and grassroots football more than once during their teenage years. Leaving an academy does not prevent future recruitment opportunities.

**Q: How many kids are at each stage?**

A: Squad sizes vary significantly by academy category, age group and club strategy. Foundation Phase age groups are usually smaller than older academy groups, while Professional Development Phase squads are more selective. Rather than relying on national averages, parents should ask the academy how many players are in the age group, how often reviews happen and how many players typically progress to the next stage.
