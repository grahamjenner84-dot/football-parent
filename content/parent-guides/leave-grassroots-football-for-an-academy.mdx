Should My Child Leave Grassroots Football For An Academy?

The message comes through on a Tuesday evening, usually while you're making dinner. A coach or academy coordinator wants to invite your child for a trial, or - if you've already been through that process - they're offering a place. Most parents feel a surge of pride in that moment, and that reaction is completely understandable as you've likely invested a lot of time and effort to get to this point.

But the weeks after that message are when the real thinking needs to happen. Because accepting an academy offer is not simply upgrading to a better version of what your child already does. It changes their schedule, their social world, your family's weekends, and sometimes their relationship with the sport itself. For some children it's genuinely the right move. For others, and this is often said quietly, it isn't. 

If you're still at the trial stage rather than holding an offer, our guide to [what happens at academy trials](/academy-trials/what-happens-at-academy-trials) 
covers what to expect and how to assess what you're seeing.

## Academy Vs Grassroots

The first thing worth understanding is what academy football actually involves compared to grassroots, because the gap is wider than just training quality.

**Grassroots football** in England varies enormously. At its best, it's a well-organised local club with a decent coach, a proper pitch, and a group of children who've been playing together since Under-7s. At its worst, it's a well-meaning parent with a bag of cones and no real coaching education trying to manage 10 kids on a waterlogged public park on a freezing Sunday morning. Most grassroots experiences sit somewhere between the two.

What grassroots generally does offer is a sense of belonging to a community. Your child knows the other kids often because its his school friends or you heard about the team from people you know. For you, there is a social element as you know the other parents. The football is the point, but it's not the only point.

Some families consider joining the [Junior Premier League](/parent-guides/what-is-the-junior-premier-league) before making the jump into academy football, although it isn't an academy pathway in itself.

**Academy football** from Under-9 upwards involves considerably more structured commitment. Most academies run a minimum of two training sessions per week, with those sessions increasing in frequency as players get older. There are fixtures, cup competitions, development centres, and at the older age groups, significant additional demands. By Under-15 and Under-16, full-time academy programmes in the professional game are effectively a part-time job for the player and a logistical operation for the family.

Beyond the time commitment, there's the matter of registration. Once a child is registered with a professional academy, their football activities are governed by FA and club rules - they cannot simply drift between teams or play in their local grassroots league as they might have done before.

It's also worth understanding the difference between a full academy registration and a development centre place, which carry different levels of commitment and different rules around registration. Our guide to [development centres vs academies](/academy-pathway/development-centres-vs-academies) covers this in detail.

It's also worth being clear about one structural reality: the majority of children who enter academy programmes at younger age groups (Under-9 to Under-12) do not go on to professional football. Academies release players regularly at review points, and the pathway narrows sharply at each stage. This doesn't make the experience worthless, but it does mean that treating the initial offer as the start of a guaranteed professional career is unrealistic.

## Potential Benefits

**Coaching quality** in Category 1 and 2 academies is typically far above what's available in grassroots football. Coaches are generally licensed to higher levels, work with smaller groups, and operate within a structured development curriculum. Sessions are designed around specific skills and are progressively built over weeks and months rather than being ad-hoc.

**Training intensity and environment** means a child is working regularly with other high-level players. The challenge is greater, the standard of training partner is higher and the level of focus required from the children is noticeably different. For children who have outgrown their local grassroots environment and are not being sufficiently challenged, this can be genuinely transformative.

**Individual feedback** The best academy coaches treat individual feedback as an ongoing conversation, not an annual review. When it works well, a parent knows exactly what their child is being asked to improve, what they did well last week, and what the focus is for the next block of sessions. Some academy environments genuinely deliver this. Others hand parents a one-page document at a review meeting and call it a development plan. Before committing, it's worth asking how feedback is actually delivered week to week - not just what the process looks like on paper.

**Exposure to the game's culture and standards** - One thing parents often notice is how quickly standards become normalised. Players are expected to arrive prepared, listen carefully, and train with intensity from the first minute. A child who drifts through grassroots warm-ups can find the transition quite a shock. Some thrive on that structure. Others find it overwhelming initially.

## Potential Downsides

The downsides of academy football are less often discussed, but they're equally real.

**Travel demands** are significant for many families. Academy training venues are not always local, and the sessions happen on weekday evenings after school. A child travelling an hour each way to training three nights a week, while managing school work and trying to maintain some kind of social life, is under considerable pressure. This is a logistical burden that falls on the whole family, not just the child.

**Time cost and lost activities.** The commitment level of academy football often means children have to give up other sports, other clubs, and activities they previously enjoyed. For younger children particularly, this is worth considering carefully. There is big debate on long-term athlete development vs early specialisation in a single sport. Some argue early specialisation is associated with higher burnout rates and is not necessary for elite development. A child who is playing football, swimming, doing athletics, and playing tennis at Under-10 may be developing athletic foundations that serve them better than exclusive football training at the same age. 

But the other side of the argument is in the early years you are laying the foundations and its hard to catch up. Like anything balance is important. Finding sports that add different physical elements will contribute positively to them being a well rounded athlete. For example, Judo would help them feel more comfortable with contact, swimming with overall conditioning and core strength, tennis with agility. But if they don't spend significant time with a ball at their feet at a young age they will struggle to catch up. 

Spending time with the ball doesn't need to be physical taxing. Dribbling around the house, ball against the wall or just practicing tricks/ball juggling are all giving them touches and helping them improve without increasing the physical toll.

**Pressure and psychological impact.** Academy environments are quietly competitive in a way that's hard to prepare children for. Players aren't formally ranked against each other but children are perceptive. They notice who gets pulled aside for extra work or who is being used for examples. They notice who's being praised in sessions or who gets more match time. They notice when the squad numbers shift at a review point and one of their teammates doesn't come back after half-term.

Some children find this motivating. They rise to it, train harder, and grow. Others carry a low-level anxiety for months that slowly drains the enjoyment out of something they used to love. The tricky part is that children often don't tell you which category they're in - particularly boys, and particularly if they feel that admitting they're struggling means letting you down.

Watch for changes in how your child talks about training. Are they still excited to go? Do they talk about it afterwards? Are they sleeping and eating normally? If you look out for these signs you are more likely to pick up how your child is feeling about it than them telling you. For my own son, when he joined a session he wasn't enjoying as much he started to say things like his ankle hurt or knee was sore (having just been running around the house) either as a way to reduce expectations or to try and not attend. 

But its not just the children, several academy parents I've spoken to said they underestimated how emotionally invested they would become. The football belongs to the child, but rejection, release and selection decisions can affect parents almost as much.

**The release point.** Release from an academy is something almost every academy family experiences eventually - the pathway is narrow by design, and the numbers don't lie. But almost no one feels prepared for it when it happens.

The way it tends to go: a meeting is requested, sometimes at short notice. A coach or academy manager explains that the club won't be renewing for the following season. There's usually some language about how hard the decision was and how much potential your child has. 

What happens next depends a lot on how the whole experience has been framed from the start. Children who've been told repeatedly that they're "academy players" as a core part of who they are tend to find release significantly harder than those whose identity was never allowed to become entirely wrapped up in it. 

This isn't about lowering ambition. Our guide to [understanding academy release](/academy-pathway/understanding-academy-release) covers how to support your child after an academy release.

If your child is released, the football doesn't have to stop. Most grassroots clubs are glad to have returning players, and many children who've been through an academy - even a difficult one - come back to grassroots with better technical foundations than their peers. The environment was hard, but the development was real. For some this return to grassroots reinvigorates their love of the game. Having been judged or placed near the bottom of their academy group suddenly they are valued and a central part of the team and you can see their self belief flourish.

**Grassroots clubs lose good players and notice.** It's also worth being aware that leaving a grassroots club where your child is valued, connected, and happy carries social costs. These are real relationships that sometimes don't survive the transition. From my own experience I think its important that you try and make sure that any decision you make is done with the child involved. Sometimes as a parent its your job to stretch them but others you need to recognise they might not be ready or that this might be your dream and not theirs. So before pulling them out of their grassroots team where they are bouncing out of bed every day to play for, speak with them and check this is what they want.

## Questions Parents Should Ask

Before making the decision, it's worth working through these questions carefully - ideally together with the child, in an age-appropriate way.

**What category is the academy?** The difference in standard and resource 
between a [Category 1 academy](/academy-pathway/what-is-eppp) and a Category 3 or 4 is significant. A Category 1 academy operates with full-time professional staff, purpose-built facilities and structured EPPP curricula. A category 3 or 4 may be a part-time operation with shared facilities and less coaching continuity. Both can offer good development, but they are not the same thing.

**How far is the travel, and how often?** Work out the realistic weekly hours involved, travel included, before agreeing. What does a typical week look like? What happens during school exam periods? It's also worth knowing that under FA and Premier League EPPP rules, academies can only register players who live within a set travel time of the training ground - typically 60 minutes at younger age groups, increasing as players get older. If a club is recruiting your child from outside that radius, it's worth asking how they're managing that within the rules.

**What is the release rate at this age group?** It's a reasonable question to ask the academy directly. Any well-run academy should be transparent about this. It helps set realistic expectations.

**Who specifically will be coaching my child?** Academy coaching quality is uneven. The head of academy may be excellent; the Under-9 lead may be a part-time coach learning his trade. Ask who runs the sessions, what their qualifications are, and how long they've been there. Staff turnover in academy coaching is high. 

**What is your child's own feeling about leaving their current club?** If your child has strong friendships at their current club and is genuinely happy there, that matters. Pushing your child to move can backfire as they might not be ready and might need time to mature and be ready to take the next step. With my son, I would sometimes take him to sessions where the level was noticeably higher. My expectation was he'll rise to the occassion but many times he would shrink into his shell, some kids prefer being the big fish in a small pond.

Many parents assume accepting an academy place automatically means leaving grassroots football. In reality, the rules vary depending on the player's age and registration status. Our guide explains [when academy players can still play grassroots football](/academy-pathway/can-academy-players-play-grassroots-football).

**Is your child motivated by the challenge or are they happy as they are?** An ambitious child who has outgrown their current environment is likely to thrive. A content child who is enjoying football and developing at a reasonable pace may not need the disruption - and may find that the academy environment, with its competitive pressure and high demands, doesn't suit them as well as their existing one.

**What happens to your child's football if they're released?** An uncomfortable question to ask, but one that reveals a lot about how the club treats families. A good academy will answer this honestly. Is there a pathway back to grassroots? Will their old club take them back? Planning for this possibility isn't pessimistic - it's responsible.

## FAQ

**Does academy football guarantee a better development pathway than grassroots?**
Not automatically. The quality of coaching varies between academies, and a well-coached grassroots environment can serve a child better than a poorly managed one. I've seen children leave grassroots for an academy and improve dramatically because they needed a more demanding environment. I've also seen children become less confident because they went from being one of the strongest players in their local team to one of the weakest in an academy group. The coaching mattered, but so did how the child handled that change. The label "academy" doesn't guarantee quality, it could mean better coaching but if your child doesn't build a good connection with the coach that can also mean they learn less. From my own experiene, I have seen my child grow and develop when the coach believes in him and can then be more demanding of him. Other coaches have been demanding of him without building that relationship and my son has looked disengaged. 

**My child was offered a place at a Category 3 academy. Is it worth the commitment?**
It depends on the specifics. Category 3 academies vary in quality, but many offer genuine developmental value. The key questions are: is the coaching good, what is the realistic travel commitment, and is the child motivated? A Category 3 academy that's local, well-run, and keeps your child developing happily may be exactly right. One that requires three hours of travel per session and causes school difficulties may not be.

**Can my child return to grassroots if academy doesn't work out?**
In most cases, yes. The FA has processes for registration changes, and most grassroots clubs welcome returning players, particularly good ones. It's worth checking your current club's attitude before making the transition, and maintaining those relationships through the process.

**My child is Under-9 and been offered an academy place. Should we take it?**

It's true that most professional footballers joined academies young - [research suggests elite players typically entered academy environments between the ages of 11 and 12 on average](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fspor.2021.660167/full). So there is something to the idea that early exposure matters.

But the survivorship bias problem is real. We know the ages of those who made it. We don't have the same data on the far larger group who joined at the same age and didn't. The FA's own talent identification research found that [less than 0.5% of players signed by professional clubs at Under-9 go on to play for the first team](https://idsoccer.com/how-many-academy-players-make-it-pro/), and a [University of Essex study found just 4% of teenage academy players reach the top tier of professional football](https://www.essex.ac.uk/news/2024/06/27/just-four-per-cent-of-teenage-academy-players-make-it-to-top-tier-football).

There is also no published research showing that Under-9 entry produces better long-term outcomes than joining at Under-11 or Under-12. Studies on deliberate practice and athlete development consistently find that accumulated time with the ball matters more than when the formal academy journey began - which reframes the question from "should we join now?" to "what does joining now actually give us that waiting two years wouldn't?"

That question cuts both ways. If the academy is local, the coaching is genuinely good, and your child is excited about it, the answer might be: quite a lot. A well-run Foundation Phase programme exposes children to structured coaching, higher-level training partners, and a development environment that a typical grassroots club can't replicate. For a child who is ready for that and thriving in it, joining at Under-9 can be a positive experience regardless of where it ultimately leads.

The case for waiting, or at least thinking carefully, is strongest when the logistics are heavy. If you're being asked to drive an hour each way three nights a week for a nine-year-old, the time cost is significant and the opportunity cost is real. That same time spent playing freely, working on the ball at home, or participating in other sports is not wasted development, it may be exactly the right foundation for that age. What you're being offered at Under-9 is an assessment of current ability, not a prediction of future potential. Children develop at very different rates through this age group, physically, technically, and emotionally. A child who looks exceptional at nine often looks ordinary at twelve, and vice versa.

So rather than a yes or no, the honest answer is: it depends on the specific offer, not the category of offer. A local, well-run academy with good coaches and manageable sessions is worth serious consideration. A long-distance commitment with significant disruption to school and family life, for a nine-year-old who isn't particularly pushing for it themselves, deserves more scrutiny than the excitement of the offer sometimes allows for.

**What should I tell my child's grassroots club if we're leaving for an academy?**
Depending on the relationship with the club/coach this can be uncomfortable and most parents underestimate how uncomfortable it will be. Your child's grassroots club - especially if it's a good one - will feel the loss. Coaches invest in players, other kids in the squad might be upset. The manager who's given up his Sunday mornings for three years deserves more than a WhatsApp message on a Friday night but generally you'd hope they'd be supportive and happy that they've played a part in getting your child ready for a higher level.

And maintain the relationship if you can. The grassroots coach who knows your child well may be exactly the person you want in your corner if things don't work out at the academy.
