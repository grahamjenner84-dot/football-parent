This article is not about the single bad match. If your child had a tough game last Sunday and you are looking for advice on the car journey home, the [what to say after football matches](/parent-guides/what-to-say-after-football-matches) guide covers that in detail.

This is for something different. It is for the parent who has watched their child go through three or four difficult matches in a row. Who has noticed that training has stopped being something their child looks forward to. Who has heard "I don't want to go" on a Tuesday evening and is not sure whether to push or to pause. Who has started wondering whether the environment their child is in is actually working for them.

## When It's More Than One Bad Match

A single poor performance is normal. A pattern is something different.

The signs that a child is going through a genuine confidence dip rather than a rough patch are usually visible if you know what to look for:

- They stop talking about football at home, even when they used to talk about it constantly
- They begin making excuses to miss training sessions they previously never missed
- They go quiet on the journey to matches rather than being animated
- Their body language on the pitch changes: less willing to receive the ball, more likely to play the safe pass, quicker to look towards the touchline after mistakes
- They come off the pitch not frustrated but flat, which is often a more concerning sign than anger

Frustration after a bad game usually means a child still cares and still believes they can do better. Flatness can mean they are starting to disengage. From experience it can be quite hard to know how to approach it as you are often as invested in their football enjoyment (or sometimes it feels even more) than your child.

This can be harder when you are both the parent and the coach, because your child may not know which version of you they are speaking to after the game. We cover that balance in our guide to [how to become a football coach](/parent-guides/how-to-become-a-football-coach).

## Why Sustained Poor Form Happens

There is rarely a single cause. In most cases, a run of poor performances in a young footballer comes from one or more of the following:

**Physical changes**

Growth spurts affect coordination, balance and physical confidence. A child who was comfortable in their body at nine can feel genuinely awkward at eleven because their limbs have changed faster than their motor skills have caught up. The Premier League has invested in [bio-banding programmes](https://www.premierleague.com/en/footballandcommunity/youth-development/eppp) that group players by physical maturity rather than birth year, specifically because this period is so disruptive to development. It passes, but it can take a full season, and in the meantime the drop in physical confidence is real and visible.

**Moving up an age group**

The step from under-tens to under-elevens, or from under-twelves to under-thirteens, can be significant. The physical and tactical gap between year groups increases as players get older. A child who was comfortable at their previous level can find the new level genuinely difficult for a full season before they adjust. This is particularly common at development centre level, where the quality of players around them is already higher than grassroots.

**Increased competition for places**

At development centres and academies, the group of players around your child is not static. New players join. Others develop quickly. A child who was one of the stronger players in the group six months ago may now be competing much harder for game time or recognition. That shift in status is difficult to process at any age.

**Social dynamics at the club**

This one gets underestimated. Children who feel excluded, ignored or unsupported by teammates often play worse. If your child has fallen out with someone in the group, if the social dynamics have shifted, or if there is a coach whose communication style does not work for them, it will show on the pitch before it shows in conversation. Sometimes asking "how are things with the other lads?" tells you more than asking about football. When my son first got into a Development Centre he knew none of the other boys. As he was naturally quite reserved you could see he was uncomfortable at first and it did impact his confidence.

**Something unrelated to football entirely**

School pressure, friendships, family stress and tiredness all affect how a child performs. If your child is carrying something outside of football, you may see it on the pitch before they mention it at home. Sometimes asking "how's everything going generally?" tells you more than any football question.

## What Not To Do When Confidence Is Low

The instincts that feel most helpful in this situation are often the ones that make it worse.

**More training**

The impulse to add extra sessions, book additional coaching or increase practice time when confidence is low is understandable but frequently counterproductive. A child who is already struggling does not usually need more repetitions of the thing they are finding hard. They need the pressure to reduce, not increase. There is more on this in our guide to [how much training is too much](/football-development/how-much-training-is-too-much). I've made this mistake at times and you are just compounding the lack of enjoyment by being more intense. Much better to either do nothing and let them have a break, or to try and create fun games and challenges that make football fun again.

**YouTube analysis**

Sitting down with your child to watch footage of their mistakes, or showing them professional players doing the things they are currently failing at, rarely helps. VEO and the like are awesome as a parent but probably a nightmare for many children who don't want blow by blow analysis of their game when they get home. It draws more attention to the gap between where they are and where they want to be. That gap is already visible to them.

**Increased touchline commentary**

As a coach, I have watched children's body language change the moment they hear their name called from the touchline during a period of poor form. It tightens them. Even 'come on, you've got this' lands as scrutiny when a child is already watching themselves too closely. The instinct is to encourage. The effect is often the opposite. Children in a low-confidence period are often more sensitive to being watched and called to, not less. Calling their name every time they receive the ball can feel like scrutiny even when it is intended as support.

**Public praise designed to rebuild confidence**

Telling a child loudly and repeatedly that they were brilliant when they clearly were not does not rebuild confidence. It teaches them that your feedback is not reliable. They already know how they played. The FA's own research found that [9 in 10 children play better with positive encouragement](https://www.thefa.com/get.involved/respect/we-only-do-positive), which is the basis of their We Only Do Positive Respect campaign. Honest, measured responses land better than exaggerated ones.

## When To Talk To The Coach

Not every difficult period requires a conversation with the coach. But some do, and knowing when to have it matters.

A conversation with the coach is reasonable when:

- Your child's playing time has reduced significantly and there has been no explanation
- Your child has told you something specific about the training environment that concerns you
- The pattern has gone on long enough (typically more than six to eight weeks) that you genuinely do not know what is driving it
- Your child is asking you to speak to the coach themselves

When you do have that conversation, the framing matters. This is not a complaint and should not feel like one. The most useful approach is to ask questions rather than make statements.

"I've noticed he's been finding it harder recently. Is there anything you're seeing from your end that might help me understand what's going on?"

That question opens a conversation. It positions you as someone trying to support the coach's work rather than challenge it. As a coach myself, when a parent comes to me with questions rather than complaints, it changes the conversation entirely. If the response is defensive or dismissive, take that seriously. A coach who cannot engage with a calm, well-framed question from a parent is telling you something about how they are likely responding to your child when things are difficult.

## When To Consider A Break Or A Change

This is the question parents are often reluctant to ask out loud because it feels like giving up.

The "I don't want to go" moment on a Tuesday evening is one of the harder calls. A single reluctant training session is usually worth gently pushing through - most children are glad they went. But if it is happening most weeks, consistently, that pattern is telling you something the football itself is not.

A short break from football is worth considering when your child is showing signs of genuine burnout: not just tiredness after matches, but a persistent reluctance that has lasted several weeks and is affecting their enjoyment of the game broadly. A two or three week break, framed to your child as a rest rather than a consequence, often resets things in a way that pushing through does not.

A club change is worth considering when the environment itself appears to be the problem. Not the football, but the specific club, coach or group dynamic. If your child comes home from training at a different mood than they arrive, consistently, over a sustained period, the environment may not be right for them at this point in their development. Children develop better in some environments than others, my own son has had some really demanding coaches that have got the best out of him as he feels important. He's had other demanding coaches where you can see him shrink under their expectations. Children also develop and change so the right club at seven is not necessarily the right club at twelve.

What a club change does not fix is a confidence dip that is coming from internal sources: a growth spurt, a difficult age-group transition, anxiety that predates the football. Moving clubs in that situation often just relocates the problem.

If your child is at academy or development centre level and you are worried about the implications of stepping back, our guide to [understanding academy release](/academy-pathway/understanding-academy-release) covers what that process typically looks like and what options remain afterwards.

## When They Say They Want To Quit

Take it seriously without acting on it immediately.

A child who says "I want to quit football" after a difficult match is almost always expressing how they feel in that moment rather than making a considered decision about their future in the game. The worst response is to immediately agree and start looking for other activities. The second worst is to dismiss it entirely.

The better response is to acknowledge it and slow things down.

"Okay. Tell me more about that. Is it football generally, or is it something specific that's been hard recently?"

That question signals that you are taking them seriously, which reduces the emotional pressure behind the statement. And it helps you work out what is actually going on.

If they say they want to quit after every difficult match but are animated and keen again by Wednesday, you are probably looking at post-match emotion rather than a genuine change of feeling. If they are saying it consistently across different moods, across several weeks, and with specificity about what they do not enjoy, it is worth treating as genuine.

Children who truly fall out of love with football usually show it before they say it. By the time they are asking to stop, the signs have typically been there for a while.

## What Your Response Teaches Them

How you handle this period will stay with your child longer than the poor form itself.

A parent who responds to their child's worst football period with steadiness, reduced pressure and genuine curiosity teaches something specific: that difficult periods are manageable, that your relationship with them is not conditional on performance, and that stepping back is sometimes the right call rather than a failure.

That is not easy. You have driven to early Sunday morning matches in the rain and watched your child struggle. The investment is visible to them too, which is partly why this period is hard for everyone. But the child's experience of the game is more important than the investment, and what they carry forward from this period depends largely on what you model during it.

The parents I have seen handle this well are not the ones who had the right words. They are the ones who stayed calm when it would have been easy not to, kept showing up without adding pressure, and trusted the process even when it was not visible.

> **Football Parent note:** A sustained dip in form is not a verdict on where your child will end up. Some of the most significant development happens during the periods that look, from the outside, like regression. During these periods you just need to support your child. For me that's meant trying to make football fun outside of training, whether that's been coming up with random games in the garden or just messing around with a ball.

## FAQ

**My child used to love football and now seems to dread it. What's changed?**

The most common causes are a change in the environment (new age group, new coach, shift in social dynamics), physical changes that have affected their comfort on the ball, or an increase in pressure from outside. Sometimes it is a combination. Start by asking open questions about what specifically feels different, rather than about performance.

**Should I reduce training or increase it when confidence is low?**

In most cases, reduce. More repetition of the thing a child is currently struggling with adds pressure rather than removing it. A lighter week, or a session that is purely unstructured and enjoyable, often does more for confidence than an extra coaching session. See our guide on [how much training is too much](/football-development/how-much-training-is-too-much).

**My child is being left out by teammates. How does that affect performance?**

Significantly. Social belonging within a team affects how freely children play. A child who feels isolated or excluded within their group will often play more cautiously, make fewer forward runs and avoid situations where mistakes might draw attention. If you suspect social dynamics are a factor, that is worth addressing directly, either through a conversation with your child or with the coach, depending on the severity.

**At what point should I speak to the coach?**

If the pattern has lasted more than six to eight weeks, if playing time has dropped without explanation, or if your child has told you something specific about the training environment. Approach it as a question rather than a complaint, and ask what they are observing rather than leading with your own assessment.

**Is it normal for academy or development centre players to go through this?**

Yes, and it is more common than most parents realise. The standard at development centre and academy level is high, and the competition within groups changes as players develop at different rates. A child who is finding it hard within a strong development environment is not necessarily falling behind. They may simply be in a group that has raised the level around them. That is not always a comfortable place to be, but it is often a productive one.
