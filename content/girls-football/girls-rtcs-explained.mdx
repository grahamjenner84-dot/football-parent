# Girls' RTCs Explained: What Parents Need to Know

If your daughter has shown ability in football and you've started researching development pathways, you'll quickly encounter the term RTC — Regional Talent Centre. RTCs are central to how girls' football talent is identified and developed in England, but they work differently from the boys' academy structure, and the terminology can cause confusion.

This guide explains what RTCs are, how they fit into the broader girls' football pathway, and what families should understand before getting involved.

---

## What Is an RTC?

An RTC — Regional Talent Centre — is a licenced programme, typically run by or in partnership with a professional club or football organisation, that identifies and develops talented female players within a defined geographic area.

RTCs are part of the FA's formal talent pathway for girls' football in England. They are not the same as commercial coaching programmes that might use similar language. A genuine RTC holds an FA licence and operates within a defined structure.

The purpose of RTCs is to provide talented girls with access to high-quality coaching and development in a professional environment, at an earlier stage than formal Women's Super League or Women's Championship academy registration.

---

## How Girls' RTCs Differ From Boys' Academy Programmes

The girls' and women's football pathway has developed somewhat independently from the boys' structure, and the terminology reflects this.

In the boys' game, the EPPP framework governs everything from pre-academy to Category 1 academies. Development centres sit informally below this structure and are not formally regulated in the same way.

In the girls' game, RTCs occupy a more formal, licenced role. They are part of the FA's tiered development structure for female players and are subject to FA standards.

Another key difference is scale. The women's professional game, while growing rapidly, is smaller than the men's game in England. This means the pathway is less densely populated — there are fewer clubs, fewer registered academy places, and fewer RTCs than there are boys' academies. In practice, this means less scouting saturation in some areas, but also fewer opportunities at the top end.

## How RTCs Fit Into the Girls' Development Pathway

The girls' talent pathway in England moves broadly through these stages:

- **Grassroots football** — local clubs, school football, girls' teams
- **RTC** — Regional Talent Centre, licenced development environment, typically from under-9 to under-12
- **Academy** — formally registered with a professional women's or girls' club, usually from under-9 or under-12 depending on the club
- **Women's development / under-21 programmes** — connecting to senior women's football
- **Professional women's football** — WSL, Women's Championship, and beyond

RTCs serve as a bridge between grassroots football and formal academy registration. They identify players who may not yet be ready for an academy, or who come from areas without a nearby club running a formal academy programme.

## What Age Do Girls Enter RTCs?

This varies by club and RTC, but most programmes begin from under-9 or under-10 level. Some RTCs focus on the foundation age groups (U9–U12); others cover older age groups too.

It is worth asking any RTC you engage with exactly what age groups they cover and what happens to players at the end of their time in the programme.

## How Girls Are Identified for RTCs

Identification routes broadly mirror the boys' pathway, with some differences:

**Scouting at grassroots level** remains the primary route. With the growth of girls' grassroots football in England, there are more players to scout than at any previous point. Scouts attend girls' league matches, schools tournaments, and regional festivals.

**Representative football** — county and regional girls' squads — is an important filter. Players selected for county-level football are more visible to clubs and RTCs.

**Direct approaches or open days** are also common, particularly for RTCs that cover areas with lower scouting density. Some RTCs run open assessment days.

**School football** still plays a role. Schools with strong girls' football programmes, or PE staff who are connected to local women's football, can be a referral route.

For more on scouting and trials in girls' football, our guide on [girls' football trials](/girls-football/girls-football-trials) covers this in detail.

## Progression From RTC to Academy

Not all RTC players progress into formal academy registration. As with the boys' pathway, RTC programmes include players at a range of levels — some who may go on to professional careers, others for whom the RTC is the peak of their development pathway.

Progression from an RTC to an academy depends on:

- The player's development during their time in the RTC
- Available spaces in the club's formal academy at the relevant age group
- The extent to which the RTC and the academy are integrated at that club
- The club's philosophy around when to formally register players

Some clubs run RTCs that are explicitly designed as feeders to their girls' academy. Others run RTCs as broader development environments.

---

## RTC Costs and Commitment

RTCs vary in what they charge. Some are free of charge; others may have nominal fees to cover costs. The more formal and FA-licenced a programme, the more likely it is to operate with transparent fee structures.

Commitment varies too. Some programmes expect weekly attendance; others meet fortnightly. As with any youth development programme, it is worth understanding the commitment level before agreeing to join.

---

## What Makes a Good RTC Environment

Whether in the boys' or girls' game, the qualities that characterise a well-run development programme are broadly similar:

- Structured sessions with a clear technical focus
- Coaches who are qualified and experienced in the girls' game
- An environment where players feel safe to make mistakes and express themselves
- Clear and honest communication with families about the programme and its outcomes
- A positive relationship with grassroots football, rather than dismissiveness of it

## Common Questions From Parents

**Can my daughter attend an RTC and still play grassroots football?**
Yes, in most cases. At pre-academy age, children are generally free to participate in grassroots football alongside RTC programmes. Once formally registered with an academy, there may be some restrictions on playing for outside clubs in certain competitions.

**What happens if my daughter is released from an RTC?**
Release from an RTC does not mean the end of a footballing pathway. Players leave and return to grassroots football regularly. Many go on to trial at other clubs or progress through the pathway at a different club later. Our article on [late developers in girls' football](/girls-football/late-developers-in-girls-football) covers the longer view.

**Is an RTC the same as an academy?**
No. An RTC is a licenced talent development programme. A girls' academy refers to a formal club programme, usually attached to a professional women's side. The two terms describe different — though sometimes overlapping — parts of the pathway.

---

## Summary

RTCs are a meaningful and formal part of the girls' football talent pathway in England. For families of girls who are developing quickly in the game, understanding how RTCs fit into the broader picture helps manage expectations and make better decisions.

As always, the most important question is whether your daughter is enjoying football, developing as a player, and thriving in the environment she is in.
