# Football Parent — Fact-Checking, Sourcing, EEAT & Style Rules

Read this file before finalising any article. It governs accuracy, tone and
the "does this sound AI-written" check.

## When to fact-check

Verify current guidance (don't rely on memory) if the article touches:
academy pathways, FA rules, talent ID systems, RTCs/ETCs/PGAs or academy
structures, youth formats, heading guidance, safeguarding, coaching
qualifications, recruitment ages, women's/girls' pathways, sports science
claims, child development claims, injury prevention, Relative Age Effect,
bio-banding.

If the article is primarily opinion/practical parent advice, only fact-check
claims that could materially affect accuracy or a reader's decisions. Don't
over-research evergreen confidence-building, matchday behaviour, or general
communication advice.

Use web search for anything in the list above where the position could have
changed. If terminology has changed, explain the CURRENT system first, and
label older systems as historical context (e.g. "RTCs were previously part of
the FA's girls' pathway. They have now been replaced by ETCs and PGAs.").
Never write as if a deprecated system is still current.

## Source priority

1. The FA
2. England Football
3. Premier League
4. UEFA
5. FIFA
6. NSPCC / CPSU safeguarding guidance
7. UK Coaching
8. Peer-reviewed sports science research
9. Peer-reviewed child development research

Avoid: SEO blogs, opinion articles, marketing content, AI-generated summaries,
forums, social media posts — unless no better source exists, and the
limitation is stated explicitly.

## Evidence attribution

Any claim referencing research, studies, psychologists, sports scientists,
coaching guidance, safeguarding guidance, governing body recommendations,
injury prevention, child development, talent development or academy research
must have a source link in the same sentence or immediately after it.

Never write "research shows", "studies prove", "experts agree", "psychologists
say", "sports science tells us", "coaches know that" without a linked source.
If no source is available: soften the claim, label it as an observation, or
cut it.

Prefer "Research suggests..." over "Research proves...".
Prefer "Many coaches look for..." over "Coaches look for...".
Prefer "In many academy environments..." over "Academies always...".
Use may / can / often / typically / in many cases rather than absolutes.

## Unsupported claims — never include

- Unverified statistics
- Generic football myths
- "All coaches", "every academy", "most scouts" without evidence
- Anything implying academy success or progression is likely
- One pathway framed as universally better than another

## Safeguarding section — mandatory when the article covers

Academies, development centres, trials, private/1-to-1 coaching, girls'
pathways, travel/tours/residentials, paid programmes, coach-parent
communication, selection/release/high-pressure environments.

Use a heading like `## Safeguarding and parent checks` or
`### Safeguarding questions parents should ask`. Keep it calm and practical,
not alarmist. Cover: knowing the designated safeguarding lead, how to report
a concern, how coaches are vetted, communication policy with children, photo/
video/social media policy, prioritising a safe environment over promises of
progression.

Link once to the most relevant of:
- [England Football Safeguarding](https://www.englandfootball.com/participate/behaviour/safeguarding)
- [The FA Safeguarding](https://www.thefa.com/football-rules-governance/safeguarding)
- [NSPCC Keeping Your Child Safe in Sport](https://www.nspcc.org.uk/keeping-children-safe/outside-the-home/sports-clubs/)
- [NSPCC Child Protection in Sport Unit](https://thecpsu.org.uk/)

Only add more safeguarding links if genuinely useful — don't overuse.

## Official source linking

When the article names a specific club, academy, pathway, governing body
programme or competition, link the name to its official page (club pathway
page, FA/England Football/Premier League/UEFA/FIFA page). Link behind natural
text, never as a bare URL.

## AI-slop removal — cut or rewrite any sentence that

- Could apply to any sport, not just football
- Is generic parenting advice with no football specifics
- Repeats a point already made
- Feels written for search engines rather than readers

Banned phrases (cut or rewrite on sight): "Every child is different", "It's
important to remember", "Ultimately", "At the end of the day", "The key thing
is", "In today's game", "Football is a journey", "Success looks different for
everyone", "There is no one-size-fits-all answer", "Trust the process",
"Focus on the process not the outcome", "Stay positive", "Keep supporting
them", "Believe in yourself", "Hard work always pays off".

If a paragraph could be dropped unmodified into a tennis, rugby, cricket or
swimming article, rewrite it to be football-specific.

## Originality requirement

Every article needs 5 football-specific observations, 3 parent
misconceptions, and 3 practical football examples — things a parent would be
unlikely to find in a generic AI article. Draw on: development centre
realities, academy recruitment realities, coaching observations, common
parent mistakes, gaps between what parents expect and what actually happens.
Do not invent personal anecdotes — write with the voice of someone
experienced around these environments, not a first-person storyteller.

## EEAT review — before finalising, remove any statement that

Sounds more certain than the evidence supports, promises outcomes, implies a
pathway guarantees progression, exaggerates opportunity, overstates research,
presents opinion as fact, or presents a trend as universal truth.

## FAQ rule

Only include FAQs that add genuinely new information not already covered
elsewhere in the article. Cut any FAQ that duplicates an earlier section.

## Style

- British English throughout.
- No em dashes — use full stops, commas, colons, semicolons or parentheses.
- No markdown pipe tables, ever. Use bullet comparisons, numbered steps,
  pros/cons blocks, or stacked "Option A vs Option B" sections instead —
  mobile-first, no horizontal overflow.
- No academic-style citations like [1] or Harvard referencing.
- No American terminology, no cheesy football clichés, no hype/clickbait.
- Tone: calm, realistic, trustworthy, parent-first — like an experienced
  football parent or coach explaining things honestly, not an SEO content
  farm or motivational blog.
- Never promise academy success or professional outcomes.

## Final checks before output

1. Does every claim needing evidence have a linked source?
2. Does every H2 appear in the `sections` frontmatter array?
3. Is a safeguarding section present if the topic requires one?
4. Are all internal links from references/valid-urls.md or the current batch?
5. Has the search intent been fully answered — would the reader need to
   Google again?
6. Are there at least 5 football-specific observations, 3 misconceptions,
   3 practical examples?
